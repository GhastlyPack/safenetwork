How to Spot Fake Silver and Gold Coins

DJ Smoltz Coins & Bullion Host at Safe Network
üß≤

Counterfeit coins are a real problem, and new collectors are the most common victims. I have seen it happen too many times ‚Äî someone buys what looks like a beautiful Silver Eagle or a stunning Morgan Dollar, then finds out it is plated copper that is worth almost nothing. The good news is that catching fakes is not hard once you know what to look for. You do not need expensive equipment. You need a few simple tools, about five minutes, and the knowledge in this article.

Do Not Skip This Step

Counterfeit coins are most common on peer-to-peer marketplaces, flea markets, and estate sales where sellers may not know ‚Äî or care ‚Äî what they actually have. Always run at least two authentication tests on any coin you buy outside of a reputable dealer or graded slab.

The Four Core Authentication Tests

No single test is foolproof on its own. Sophisticated counterfeiters have learned to pass individual checks. Running all four tests together gives you a very high level of confidence before any money changes hands.

Weight Test

This is your most important tool. Every coin ever minted has a precisely documented weight, and counterfeit coins almost never match it exactly. A kitchen scale accurate to 0.1 grams is all you need ‚Äî you can find one for under $15 on Amazon.

Magnet Test

Silver and gold are not magnetic. Neither is copper, which makes up the core of most legitimate coins. Steel and iron ‚Äî the most common materials used in cheap counterfeits ‚Äî are strongly magnetic. A neodymium rare earth magnet, available for a few dollars online, will stick hard to any steel fake the moment you bring it close.

Here is how to run the test properly:

  • Hold the coin flat in one hand
  • Bring the magnet close slowly ‚Äî do not slam it onto the coin
  • A genuine silver coin will show zero attraction
  • A steel fake will pull noticeably toward the magnet or stick entirely

The Slide Test for Advanced Checking

Silver is slightly diamagnetic, meaning it actually repels magnetic fields weakly. If you tilt a coin at about 45 degrees and slide a strong magnet down its face, a genuine silver coin will slow the magnet slightly as it passes. A plated copper or zinc fake will let the magnet slide freely at full speed. This takes practice but is a useful secondary check for coins that pass the basic magnet test.

"The magnet test takes three seconds and costs nothing if you already own a magnet. There is no reason not to run it on every single coin you buy outside of a certified slab."

Sound Test

Real silver has a distinct acoustic signature. When you tap a genuine silver coin with another coin or drop it onto a hard surface, it produces a clear, bell-like ring that sustains for a moment. Base metals produce a dull, dead thud with almost no resonance.

The easiest way to learn this is to tap a known genuine Silver Eagle against another genuine coin, then listen carefully. Once you hear the real ring, fakes become obvious. You can also find audio comparisons online ‚Äî searching "silver coin ring test" on YouTube will give you dozens of examples in minutes.

The sound test is particularly useful for Morgan Dollars and Peace Dollars, where the large silver content produces a very pronounced ring that base metal fakes simply cannot replicate.

Visual Inspection

A good loupe or magnifying glass in the 5x to 10x range lets you examine details that are impossible to fake at scale. Here is what to look for:

Edge Reeding

Most silver dollars and Eagles have reeded edges ‚Äî the ridged pattern around the circumference. On genuine coins, the reeding is sharp, consistent, and evenly spaced. On fakes, reeding is often shallow, uneven, or missing sections. Run your thumbnail around the edge and feel for consistency.

Surface Texture and Luster

Genuine mint-state coins have a distinctive cartwheel luster ‚Äî a flowing, directional shine that moves as you rotate the coin under light. Plated fakes tend to have a flat, uniform shine without that depth. Circulated genuine coins have natural wear patterns on the high points of the design. Fakes often show artificial wear or strange surface textures that do not match how genuine coins age.

Design Sharpness

Cast counterfeits ‚Äî made by pouring metal into a mold ‚Äî almost always show slightly mushy or rounded design details compared to struck originals. Look closely at fine details like hair strands, feathers on Eagles, or lettering serifs. Genuine struck coins have crisp, sharp definition at these points. Casts do not.

Color and Tone

Silver has a specific gray-white color with natural toning that develops over time. Plated fakes often show a slightly different hue, or the plating starts to wear through at the high points revealing a different color underneath. Look at the rims and the highest relief points of the design first ‚Äî those are where plating fails earliest.

The magnet test is the fastest first check. Real silver is not magnetic. If a coin sticks to a magnet or shows strong magnetic attraction, it is fake. Follow up with a weight test using a scale accurate to 0.1 grams for confirmation.

Some sophisticated fakes use non-magnetic metals like copper or zinc that will pass the magnet test. This is why you should always combine multiple tests ‚Äî magnet, weight, dimensions, and sound ‚Äî rather than relying on any single method.

A genuine American Silver Eagle weighs exactly 31.103 grams and measures 40.6mm in diameter with a thickness of 2.98mm. Any significant deviation from these specs is a red flag.
